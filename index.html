The World

Forty alien spacecraft — the Big Dumb Objects — materialized in orbit seven years ago. They haven't spoken. They haven't attacked. They just hover, transmitting a signal that drives the unmedicated to madness. The government calls it the Doom. They hand out tranquilizer pills. Most people take them and learn to live with a curtain drawn over the sky.

Three teenagers from Weyburn, Saskatchewan have not. Kirk, Roland, and Louis have stolen a van, pocketed their pills, and pointed themselves west — toward the Rocky Mountains, toward the commune that has grown up beneath the nearest anchor, toward whatever truth lives at the Wedge before the government shuts it all down.

The local BDO — Lambert — was somewhere overhead even now, silent and watchful behind the blue vacancy. That's what daytime was: a curtain cast over the birdcage. After sunset the curtain dropped, revealing the BDO glittering in the dark like a newborn star.

The Runaways

Kirk
The Preacher's Son

Seventeen, pale, too careful for the road he's on. He made a deal with God: believe, if there's a sign at the Wedge. He's been waiting his whole life to find out if anyone's listening.

Roland
The Nihilist

Cynical. Reckless. Wearing a leather jacket in forty-degree heat. He says nothing matters — but he came anyway, and that's the part he can't explain.

Louis
The True Believer

Walleyed, comic-book-obsessed, convinced the BDOs are trying to say something. He's the one who swapped their pills. He needed them to hear it too.

Marlene
The Half-Cree Stranger

They pulled her from a wreck on the highway. She was already fried — already smiling. She says she saw her dead father. She says she knows where they're going, even if they don't.

From the novel

"You want to see the mountains, don't you?" Kirk asked. "Make it to the Wedge?"

"Sure."

"Alive?"

"I mean, whatever works."

"Then why are you doing this?"

"This!" Kirk gestured toward the van, the road, the waves of swaying canola. "We'll only get one shot, and you're wasting it. Any day now and they're gonna close it all off."

"I'm on vacation, man. I'm not trying to hold it together anymore."

A younger Kirk would have taken the crash as a sign — a more innocent Kirk, his faith in higher beings exerting sovereign wills not yet distorted by the arrival of the BDOs. That was the Kirk he was meant to be leaving behind. Now that the day had come, he found the act feel strained. Violent, even. Moulting creatures needed to carve themselves out of their old skin.

A Road Trip. A Reckoning.

Pneumanauts is a coming-of-age story set against the vastness of the Canadian prairie — a landscape that has always demanded answers from the sky and received only silence in return. It is a novel about faith and its abandonment, about the terror of contact and the deeper terror of what it means if no one is out there after all. It is about three boys driving toward a thing they cannot name, and the girl who already knows its name.
